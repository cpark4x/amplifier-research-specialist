Amplifier Session Story

“How Do You Know?”

Building a Storyteller specialist that proves its value,
then fixing the one thing that held it back

March 2026  ·  Session Case Study
Act 1 — The Storyteller Ships

A specialist that does something
no other specialist can.

The pipeline had a Researcher, a Formatter, an Analyzer, and a Writer. It could find information, structure it, and produce polished documents. What it couldn’t do was make anyone care.

Cognitive Mode Translation

Transforms analytical findings into narrative structure. Selects a framework (SCQA, hero’s journey, before/after) across three axes: goal, audience, tone.

Narrative Selection Record

Every included finding gets a narrative role. Every omitted finding gets a rationale. Full editorial transparency — nothing hidden.

13/13 Contract Items

Quality gate works. Boundary cases correctly return NOT MET. Output format verified line by line. The specialist is clean.

The question wasn’t whether it worked.
The question was whether it helped.

Act 2 — The Blind Test

Prove it. Blind.

A controlled A/B/C comparison using a frozen evidence pack — 8 documents, line-numbered, with strict citation rules. Three pipeline variants. Randomized X/Y/Z labels. Two independent judges per run who never saw which variant produced which memo.

Variant A

Writer only. No analysis, no narrative. Raw evidence in, memo out.

Variant B

Analyzer → Writer. Structured analysis feeding the Writer. No storytelling.

Variant C

Analyzer → Storyteller → Writer. The full pipeline with narrative framing.

Five rubric criteria: Evidence Discipline, Decision Clarity, Narrative Memorability, Actionability, Risk Honesty. Each scored 1–5 by two blind judges.

The Results

Three runs. Three different winners.

Round A (Writer) B (Analyzer) C (Storyteller)
Run 1 35.5 39.5 46.5
Run 2 42.5 39.5 41.5
Run 3 44.5 46.5 39.0

Every variant won once. That’s noise, not signal.

Act 3 — The Statistics

Cross-run aggregation: 6 scorecards, 18 data points per variant.

On every other criterion — evidence, decisions, actionability, risk —

The Deeper Signal

The Storyteller never dropped below 20.5

C — Storyteller

Mean: 21.92 (88%)
CV: 5.7%
Highest mean. Tightest confidence interval. Most consistent performer.

A — Writer-only

Mean: 20.42 (82%)
CV: 10.7%
Competitive scores look like generation luck. No structural advantage.

B — Analyzer

Mean: 18.92 (76%)
CV: 18.2%
Swings 6.5 points between runs. Too unstable for a default.

But with only 6 scorecards per variant, we’d need ~26 to reliably detect these differences. “Not significant” means “underpowered,” not “no effect.”

Act 4 — The Honest Review

Stop testing the Storyteller.
Review everything.

Instead of running more blind tests, Chris asked for something harder: an honest review of all 6 specialists and every meaningful chain combination. Not just the new one — all of them.

6 Specialists

Each rated on format compliance, content quality, guardrails, and test coverage. Honest scores, not aspirational ones.

8 Chains

Every real-world combination stack-ranked by expected usage. Scored on reliability and output quality.

The Finding

The #1 most-used chain was the most fragile. The Storyteller wasn’t the problem.

The Real Problem

The chain every user reaches for first:

Researcher
→
Writer

The Researcher’s output format was unreliable — ~1 in 3 topics produced non-canonical output. The Formatter that normalizes it existed but was optional. The Writer received garbage and dropped structural blocks.

Everyone was focused on the new specialist.
The old ones were the ones breaking.

The Fixes

Stack-rank by usage.
Fix from the top.

Fix #1

Formatter always in path. New Rule 5: the coordinator must always route through the Formatter when Researcher output feeds any downstream specialist. Invisible to the user.

Fix #2

Writer hardening. Vague audiences default to “technical decision-maker” instead of inflating. Structural blocks now mandatory regardless of input format.

Fix #3

Citation-backed comparisons. Competitive Analysis matrix now carries evidence, source URL, and tier on every line.

All 311 tests passing. Committed and pushed. Three chains projected to jump from fragile to solid.

“Projected.”

Act 5 — The Turning Point

“How do you know they are all 3 star?
Is this confirmed through testing
or theoretical?”

Five words that reframed the entire session.

The Honest Answer

Theoretical.

The ratings are my projection based on what the instruction changes should do — not confirmed through live testing.

Two live smoke tests were run. The results split:

Chain #1: Researcher → Writer

Every checkpoint hit. 9 findings in, 9 S-numbers out, 9 citations mapped. Format contracts met. 347-word brief within budget. Genuinely fixed.

Chain #3: Competitive Analysis → Writer

Good substance, completely wrong format. Zero URLs in output. No pipe-delimited matrix. The instruction changes didn’t work — the same class of problem the Researcher has. Still broken.

The Correction

The scorecard was updated. Not with projections —
with evidence.

An instruction-only fix that looks right

The Reflection

“Did we overcomplicate it?
Is it any good?”

The Storyteller itself is good. We spent more time evaluating it than fixing the one problem the first test already identified.

Three blind rounds to answer a question that one round answered:
the Storyteller → Writer handoff bleeds narrative framing into factual claims.

Act 6 — The Stitch Fix

One missing input type.

The Writer recognized researcher-output, analyst-output, analysis-output, and raw-notes. It didn’t recognize story-output. So it parsed narrative prose as factual claims — that’s the precision bleed.

The fix: one new rule — source claims from INCLUDED FINDINGS, not from story prose.

Analyzer
→
Storyteller
→
Writer (story-output)

Stage 4 guidance: “If you can’t trace it to an INCLUDED FINDING, cut it — even if it sounds good.”

The Storyteller already emits an INCLUDED FINDINGS block listing every finding it used and its narrative role. The Writer just needed to know that block exists — and to ignore the prose.

Act 7 — The Proof

10 narrative traps.
10 excluded.

The Storyteller’s prose was laced with editorial claims, evaluative judgments, and dramatic emphasis — the exact patterns that caused precision bleed before. The Writer had to resist all of them.

Traps in the prose

“Apple, Google, and Samsung have already answered that”

Editorial claim — finding says “major OEMs committed”

“The privacy posture is genuinely strong”

Evaluative — finding says “material advantage”

“These are not suggestions; they are prerequisites”

Dramatic emphasis — finding says “hard gates”

What the Writer produced

Every claim sourced to an S-numbered finding.

Used the narrative arc for structure, not as a source of facts.

Zero editorial claims, zero evaluative judgments, zero dramatic emphasis made it into the final document.
